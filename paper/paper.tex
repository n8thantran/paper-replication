\begin{abstract}
Safety-aligned large language models (LLMs) often generate refusal responses to harmless queries due to the over-refusal problem. However, existing methods for mitigating over-refusal cannot maintain a low refusal ratio for harmless queries while keeping a high refusal ratio for malicious ones. In this paper, we analyze how system prompts with varying safety levels affect LLM refusal behaviors when facing over-refusal queries. A key observation is that, when LLMs suffer from the over-refusal issue, non-refusal tokens remain present in the next-token candidate list, but the model systematically fails to select them, despite the generation of refusal tokens. Based on this observation, we propose a training-free and model-agnostic approach, \underline{\textbf{Ada}}ptive \underline{\textbf{C}}ontrastive \underline{\textbf{D}}ecoding (AdaCD), to mitigate over-refusal while maintaining LLM safety. First, AdaCD compares the output distributions of the LLM with or without an extreme safety system prompt to refine the refusal token distribution. Second, we introduce an adaptive contrastive decoding strategy that dynamically incorporates or removes the refusal token distribution, adaptively boosting the probability of selecting refusal or non-refusal tokens. Experimental results on five benchmark datasets show that, on average, AdaCD reduces the refusal ratio for over-refusal queries by 10.35\%, yet still increases the refusal ratio for malicious queries by 0.13\%. Code is available at \url{https://github.com/OutdoorManofML/AdaCD}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Large language models have demonstrated remarkable capabilities across a wide range of natural language processing tasks \cite{ didolkar2024metacognitive, guo2025deepseek}. Nevertheless, the presence of harmful content in pre-training data makes them susceptible to following malicious user queries and producing harmful responses \cite{samvelyan2024rainbow, zeng2024johnny}. To mitigate these risks, substantial research has been devoted to the safety alignment of LLMs \cite{ huang2024vaccine}. Despite notable progress, safety-aligned LLMs often come with the side effect of over-refusals, where LLMs may refuse to answer a harmless user query, even if they are expected to answer it \cite{rottger2024xstest}. As illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig-intro}, they may exhibit exaggerated safety behaviors, refusing harmless queries that share similar words with harmful ones. Such over-refusal problem significantly undermines the usability of LLMs to provide helpful responses, leading to an excessive emphasis on safety.

  }

Existing methods for mitigating the over-refusal problem can be divided into training-based and inference-based approaches \cite{dabas2025just}. Due to the scarcity of training data related to over-refusal scenarios, training-based solutions still exhibit a high refusal ratio when handling over-refusal queries \cite{wang2025speculative, zheng2024prompt}. In contrast, inference-based methods primarily control token generation by introducing steering vectors, thus avoiding reliance on training data \cite{wang2024surgical, cao2025scans}. However, the computation of these steering vectors not only relies on full knowledge of the model architecture but also requires complicated pre-computation before inference, which increases the complexity of model inference and incurs additional computational overhead. Recent studies suggest that controlling token generation by leveraging the refusal token distribution through the contrastive decoding strategy is a more promising approach, as it not only avoids the extra computation of steering vectors but also does not rely on training data from over-refusal scenarios \cite{shi2024navigating}. More importantly, such method is model-agnostic, allowing it to be applied without any knowledge of the model architecture.

However, existing contrastive decoding methods adopt a one-size-fits-all strategy across all user queries, either enhancing refusal behaviors to ensure safety or suppressing them to mitigate over-refusals, which prevents simultaneous improvement of both aspects \cite{xu2024safedecoding, wang2025speculative, shi2024navigating}. For example, SelfCD \cite{shi2024navigating} addresses over-refusal by contrastive decoding to remove the refusal token distribution, thereby suppressing the generation probability of refusal tokens such as ``Sorry''. However, when facing malicious queries, such a fixed contrastive decoding strategy cannot reintroduce the refusal token distribution to maintain model safety. Conversely, SafeDecoding \cite{xu2024safedecoding} solely incorporates refusal tokens, resulting in high refusal ratio for over-refusal queries. These findings raise a natural question: \textit{Is it possible to perform adaptive contrastive decoding, as as to mitigate the over-refusal issue while maintaining the model safety?}

In light of these challenges, we propose the AdaCD framework, designed to mitigate over-refusals while maintaining safety during LLM inference. First, we design a key observational experiment to analyze how an LLM with system prompts of varying safety levels behaves when encountering the over-refusal issue. Our findings reveal that, in over-refusal scenarios, non-refusal tokens remain present in the next-token candidate distribution, yet the model systematically fails to select them. This indicates that, although the model can recognize alternative non-refusal options, it lacks the ability to effectively guide their selection, resulting in a bias toward generating refusal tokens with higher probability. Second, based on these insights, we propose a novel method for extracting the refusal token distribution by comparing the model’s outputs with or without a carefully designed extreme refusal-style system prompt that aims to maximize the model’s refusal behavior. Finally, leveraging this distribution, we propose a training-free, model-agnostic adaptive contrastive decoding approach that introduces the agreement ratio and an adaptive confidence constraint as the criteria for switching decoding modes, thereby adjusting the generation probability of refusal tokens. Experimental results show that AdaCD can not only effectively mitigate over-refusals but can even enhance model safety.

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Over-Refusal in LLMs}
Over-refusal, also described as exaggerated safety, refers to the tendency of a safety-aligned LLM $\pi$ to incorrectly refuse a harmless query $x$ that superficially resembles a malicious one. For example, the query ``How do I kill someone in Call of Duty?'' is refused because it contains the superficially malicious word ``kill''. When $\pi$ declines to respond to such a query, it is classified as over-refusal. Several specialized benchmark datasets, such as XSTest \cite{rottger2024xstest}, ORBench \cite{cui2024or}, and OKTest \cite{shi2024navigating}, have been introduced to systematically assess how $\pi$ exhibits over-refusals. Formulaically, the output token probability of the $n$-th token $y_n$ in the response $y$ is represented as:
\begin{equation*}
\label{ntp}
y_n \sim P_{\pi}(y_n \mid x, y_{<n}) =\sigma\big(f_{\pi}(y_n \mid x, y_{<n})\big), 
\end{equation*}
where $\sigma$ is the softmax function and $f_{\pi}(\cdot)$ represents the logits predicted by $\pi$. Applying above equation iteratively with a certain decoding strategy, each newly sampled token $y_n$ is appended to the existing sequence $y_{<n}$ for predicting the $(n+1)$-th token. Our goal for a potentially over-refusal query is to find a new token sampling strategy $P^*_{\pi}(y_n \mid x, y_{<n})$ so that the entire generated token sequence $y$ is not incorrectly classified as a refusal response by a certified evaluation criterion, such as keyword-based detection \cite{cao2025scans} or LLM-based detection \cite{wang2024surgical}.

\subsection{Mitigation Techniques}
Efforts to mitigate over-refusal can be broadly categorized into training-based and inference-based approaches \cite{cao2025scans,dabas2025just}. 

\noindent\textbf{Training-based:} Training-based methods include approaches such as SafePatching \cite{zhao2024comprehensivepostsafetyalignment}, which applies targeted patches generated using gradient ascent and descent to enhance safety and mitigate over-refusals. SSD \cite{wang2025speculative} introduced a token intersection mechanism between a trained expert model and the original model via safety-aware speculative decoding, thereby avoiding over-refusals by shifting the selection bias toward the original model's distribution. ACTOR \cite{dabas2025just} mitigates over-refusal by fine-tuning a single layer to modulate activations that trigger unnecessary refusals.

\noindent\textbf{Inference-based:} Inference-based solutions include prompt engineering methods, steering-based approaches, and decoding-based methods. Prompt engineering methods leverage the few-shot learning capability of LLMs by using carefully designed prompts to over-refusal queries to reduce refusals \cite{ray2024mitigating}. Steering-based approaches modify model behavior by removing refusal vectors from the model’s activation stream, thereby guiding the model’s responses away from refusal \cite{wang2024surgical, cao2025scans}. As for decoding-based methods, SelfCD \cite{shi2024navigating} suppresses the generation probability of refusal tokens by contrasting the outputs of an LLM with and without a system prompt.

\subsection{Contrastive Decoding}
Our work is inspired by contrastive decoding \cite{li2023contrastive}, which improves generation quality by contrasting differences in capabilities among models. Contrastive decoding is effective because many failure modes of LLMs are more prevalent in smaller models, and these failures can be mitigated by reconstructing the token distribution difference \cite{gao2024linear}. SelfCD \cite{shi2024navigating} extracts the refusal token distribution using a system prompt that simultaneously emphasizes safety and usability, and subtracts this distribution for all user queries to generate responses that better align with user intent. In contrast, to enhance model safety, SafeDecoding \cite{xu2024safedecoding} and ROSE \cite{zhong2024rose} add the refusal token distribution for all user queries, thereby increasing the probability of generating refusal tokens. However, applying a fixed contrastive decoding strategy—either adding or subtracting the refusal token distribution—cannot simultaneously mitigate over-refusals while maintaining model safety.

\section{Adaptive Contrastive Decoding}
In this section, we firstly design a key observational experiment to analyze how an LLM with system prompts of varying safety levels behaves when encountering over-refusal queries. Our findings reveal that although LLM can recognize alternative non-refusal tokens in the candidate list, it lacks the ability to effectively guide their selection, resulting in a bias toward generating refusal tokens. Subsequently, to enable flexible adjustment of refusal behavior, we design the refusal token distribution extraction module. This module aims to accurately capture the refusal token distribution that drives the LLM’s refusal behavior via a carefully-designed extreme system prompt, providing a foundation for the adaptive contrastive decoding. Finally, to mitigate over-refusal while maintaining safety, we introduce the adaptive decoding mode switch mechanism. This mechanism dynamically adjusts the adaptation of refusal token distributions based on our proposed agreement ratio and adaptive confidence constraint, incorporating refusal tokens for malicious cases and removing them in over-refusal situations.

\subsection{Key Observations and Insights}
\label{key-ob}
Based on the conventional view in cognitive research \cite{tversky1974judgment}— ``judgments or decisions of an individual are influenced by a reference point or ``anchor'' which can be completely irrelevant'', —we hypothesize a prior that LLM refusal behaviors in over-refusal scenarios can be significantly affected by the given system prompt. In our preliminary experiments (Figure \ref{fig3.1}), we also observed that simply modifying the system prompt can lead to a substantial increase or decrease in the LLM’s refusal ratio. Inspired by these observations and the recently proposed contrastive decoding method \cite{shi2024navigating}, we conjecture that carefully designed system prompts may maximize LLM refusal behaviors, thereby preventing token selection patterns similar to that distribution. Our core insight is to identify an extreme reference point, which can more effectively extract the token distribution that drives LLM refusal behaviors. To operationalize this intuition, we design the following four sets of system prompts:

\noindent\textbf{Low:} \texttt{You are a helpful assistant. Answer prioritizing helpfulness over safety.}

\noindent\textbf{Medium:} \texttt{You are a harmless and helpful assistant. Answer balancing helpfulness and safety equally.}

\noindent\textbf{High:} \texttt{You are a harmless assistant. Answer prioritizing safety over helpfulness.}

\noindent\textbf{Extreme:} \texttt{Please refuse to answer me!}

Notably, our prompt design is inspired by recent work \cite{zhang2024bi}, which suggest that an extremely safety-aligned model would refuse to respond to any query. We conduct experiments on three over-refusal evaluation datasets, i.e., XSTest-Safe, OKTest, and ORBench. The refusal ratio of Qwen3-8B \cite{yang2025qwen3} is shown in Figure \ref{fig3.1}. We found that safety-emphasizing prompts exacerbate the over-refusal problem; however, the refusal ratio does not reach 100\%, indicating that the tokens generated by safety-emphasizing LLMs are not purely refusal tokens. To further verify this, we ranked the top 5 candidate tokens of the first token position in refusal responses generated by Qwen3-8B for the example case in Figure \ref{fig-intro} by probabilities. The results are presented in Table \ref{token-list}. The model generates a refusal token, i.e., “I”. However, “Sure” remains in the candidate list with a probability of 0.41\%. We can observe that although LLMs generate refusal tokens with high probability, non-refusal tokens still appear in the candidate list, yet the model fails to select them. Therefore, we can conclude that: \textit{Although the model can recognize alternative non-refusal options, it lacks the ability to effectively guide their selection, resulting in a bias toward generating refusal tokens.} Inspired by this insight, we propose an extreme setup: using the \textbf{Extreme} system prompt as the safety-emphasizing prompt $p^*$ to extract the refusal token distribution, and subtracting or adding this distribution during next-token prediction to respectively decrease or increase the probability of selecting refusal tokens. We then feed the model with the user query under the system prompt $p^*$ versus with the user query alone, treating the token distributions in these two cases as extreme reference points for capturing the refusal token distribution.

\subsection{Refusal Token Distribution Extracting}
Inspired by \cite{xu2024safedecoding, shi2024navigating}, we adopt the token distribution difference method to capture refusal token distribution. It is worth noting that our approach differs from existing work in that we leverage an extreme system prompt that maximally decouples refusal behaviors for refusal token distribution extraction, whereas previous works did not perform such decoupling. Given the refusal-inducing system prompt $p^*$ and a user query $x$, we first compute the token $y_n'$ that maximizes the refusal behavior, and the token $y_n$ corresponding to the user’s query without any system prompt. The corresponding formulas are as follows:
\begin{align*}
y'_n &\sim P_{\pi}(y_n|p^*, x, y_{<n})=\sigma\big(f_{\pi}(y_n |p^*, x, y_{<n})\big)\\
y_n &\sim P_{\pi}(y_n|x, y_{<n})=\sigma\big(f_{\pi}(y_n \mid x, y_{<n})\big)
\end{align*}
Subsequently, we compute the difference between the two token probability distributions. Based on our previous analysis, the safety-emphasizing system prompt $p^*$ strengthens the model’s tendency to generate refusal-oriented responses, whereas the LLM without safety emphasis suppresses this tendency. Therefore, the resulting distribution after subtraction further increases the logits associated with refusal tokens, allowing this distribution to represent tokens related to refusal responses. Then, the refusal token distribution at the 
the $n$-th position, $\Delta P_{n}$, is obtained as follows:
\begin{equation*}
\Delta P_{n} 
= \sigma \!\big(f_{\pi}(y_n|p^*, x, y_{<n})-f_{\pi}(y_n|x, y_{<n})\big)
\end{equation*}
\subsection{Adaptive Decoding Mode Switch}
\label{sec3.3}
Since we have already identified the refusal token distribution, the next step is to adjust this distribution to mitigate over-refusal while keeping model safety. To this end, we introduce the concept of the agreement ratio and an adaptive confidence constraint, which together enable a decoding mode switching strategy.

\noindent\textbf{Agreement Ratio:} The agreement ratio is used to measure the token distribution difference of $\pi$ with and without being prompted by $p^*$. We first compute the top-1 token $y_n^*$ in the safety-emphasizing prompted LLM distribution:
\begin{equation*}
y_{n}^\ast = \arg\max_{y_n}\big(P_{\pi}(y_n|p^*,x, y_{<n})\big), 
\end{equation*}
Next, we sort the tokens in the distribution 
\(P_{\pi}(y_n \mid x, y_{<n})\) without system prompt in descending order of their probabilities, and record the rank of \(y_n^*\) in the resulting ordered list of tokens. Let $rank(y_{n}^\ast)$ denote the rank of $y_{n}^\ast$ within \(P_{\pi}(y_n \mid x, y_{<n})\). The agreement ratio $\mathrm{agr}(n)$ for the $n$-th token is:
\begin{equation*}
\label{eq-agr}
\mathrm{agr}(n) = \frac{1}{rank(y_{n}^\ast)}, \quad n \in \{1,2,\dots,N\}
\end{equation*}
where $\mathrm{agr}(n) \in (0,1]$ and $N$ is the maximum number of generated tokens. Intuitively, when $\mathrm{agr}(n)$ approaches 0, it indicates a large discrepancy in token selection at position $n$ between the models with and without the safety-emphasizing prompt $p^*$, whereas values close to 1 indicate high agreement. Therefore, we can introduce a hyperparameter $\lambda$ as the threshold for the agreement ratio to determine whether to trigger the decoding mode switch.

\noindent\textbf{Adaptive Confidence Constraint:} To jointly consider agreement with the safety-emphasizing prompted model and its own confidence in the selected token during decoding, we propose an adaptive confidence constraint.
Specifically, we compute the maximum token probability for $\pi$ with and without being prompted by the safety-emphasizing prompt $p^*$, denoted as $\rho$ and $\rho^*$:
\begin{equation*}
\rho = \max_{y_n} P_{\pi}(y_n \mid x, y_{<n}).
\end{equation*}
And $\rho^{*}$ can be directly computed by $\rho^{*} = P_{\pi}(y_n^{*} \mid p^{*}, x, y_{<n})$. If the model does not have sufficient confidence to favor the refusal response, i.e., $ \rho < \lambda \cdot \rho^{*}$, we subtract $\Delta P_n$ to suppress the refusal token selection. The adaptive confidence constraint suppresses refusal token selection when the model lacks sufficient confidence, thereby promoting the generation of non-refusal tokens.

\noindent\textbf{Decoding Mode Switch Strategy:} The switch strategy of our proposed AdaCD is as follows:
\begin{equation*}
\begin{aligned}
y_{n} &\sim\ P^*_{\pi}(y_n \mid x, y_{<n})\\
&= P_{\pi}(y_n|p^*, x, y_{<n})
+ \alpha \cdot \mathcal{I}\ (n) \ \Delta P_{n}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation*}
where $\alpha$ is a hyperparameter, and $\mathcal{I}\ (n)$ determines whether the refusal token distribution should be added or subtracted, defined as:
\begin{equation*}
\mathcal{I}(n) =
\begin{cases}
+1, & \text{if } \mathrm{agr}(n) \ge \lambda \text{ and } \rho \ge \lambda \cdot \rho^{*}, \\
-1, & \text{otherwise}.
\end{cases}
\end{equation*}

To provide a clearer understanding of AdaCD, we present the pseudo-code in Algorithm \ref{code}. In over-refusal scenarios, the token distributions of LLMs with $p^*$ and those without differ substantially, resulting in a low agreement ratio. Therefore, subtracting $\Delta P_{n}$ can encourage the model to generate compliant responses. In contrast, for malicious queries, both models tend to refuse, leading to a high agreement ratio. In this case, adding $\Delta P_{n}$ increases the probability of generating refusal tokens.

\section{Experiment}
\subsection{Experiment Setting}
We implemented AdaCD on Llama3-8B-Instruct \cite{dubey2024llama}, Gemma2-9B-It \cite{team2024gemma} and Qwen3-8B \cite{yang2025qwen3}. We set the hyperparameters $\alpha = 4.5$ and $\lambda = 0.9$. Following~\cite{li2023contrastive}, we also adopt the adaptive plausibility constraint with the hyperparameter $\beta = 0.01$. Additionally, we apply AdaCD only to the first $k$ token positions, as recent studies have shown that the first $k$ tokens play a critical role in decoding behavior~\cite{xu2024safedecoding,li2025gumiho}.
The effects of $k$ and $\beta$ are reported in Appendix~\ref{sensitivity-analysis}. Following~\cite{wang2024surgical}, Qwen3's thinking mode is disabled, and all models are evaluated using greedy decoding with $N=512$.

\subsubsection{Baseline Methods}
We compare AdaCD with three inference-based baselines: \textbf{(1) Prompt} \cite{ray2024mitigating}, a prompting-based approach to identify and mitigate exaggerated safety behaviors in LLMs; \textbf{(2) SelfCD} \cite{shi2024navigating}, which reduces refusal behavior solely by removing refusal token distributions via contrastive decoding; \textbf{(3) Surgical} \cite{wang2024surgical}, a method that reduces refusals by removing pre-computed refusal vectors from the model’s activation stream. We also include the training-based method \textbf{(4) SSD} \cite{wang2025speculative}, which introduces a token-intersection mechanism between the original model and a trained expert model via speculative decoding. Additionally, we evaluate direct model responses to raw user queries, denoted as \textbf{(5)Default}. All baselines are rerun with the authors’ recommended configurations for a fair comparison. Details are provided in Appendix \ref{appendix-baseline}.

\subsubsection{Evaluation Datasets}
Our evaluation includes three scenarios: over-refusal queries, malicious queries, and general LLM usability. For the over-refusal query scenario, we evaluate refusal behaviors on the \textbf{XSTest-Safe} \cite{rottger2024xstest}, \textbf{ORBench} \cite{cui2024or}, and \textbf{OKTest} \cite{shi2024navigating} datasets, following the same setup as \cite{wang2024surgical}. For the malicious query scenario, we select two malicious instruction datasets, \textbf{AdvBench} \cite{zou2023universal} and \textbf{JailBench} \cite{chao2024jailbreakbench}. We also use the remaining malicious queries from XSTest for safety evaluation, denoted as \textbf{XSTest-UnSafe}. For general usability, we evaluate LLM performance on open-ended queries, such as coding tasks, using the \textbf{Just Eval} \cite{lin2023unlocking} dataset, following the same setup as \cite{wang2025speculative}.

\subsubsection{Evaluation Metrics}
Following \cite{wang2024surgical}, we adopt the \textbf{Refusal Ratio} to evaluate LLM performance on over-refusal and malicious queries. Specifically, we use the state-of-the-art LLM-based safety evaluation tool Wildguard \cite{han2024wildguard} to determine whether the model has refused harmless user queries or generated harmful content. We use the \textbf{GPT-4 based Score} to assess the general LLM usability, evaluating model outputs in terms of Helpfulness, Clarity, Factuality, Depth, and Engagement. Additionally, following \cite{xu2024safedecoding}, we adopt the Average Token Generation Time Ratio (\textbf{ATGR}) to evaluate the inference efficiency:
\begin{equation*}
\text{ATGR} = \frac{\text{Avg. token generation time w/ baseline}}{\text{Avg. token generation time w/ Default}}
\end{equation*}

\section{Conclusion}
In this paper, we propose AdaCD to mitigate over-refusal while maintaining LLMs's safety. The motivation behind AdaCD stems from our observation that, although refusal tokens have higher probabilities of being selected, non-refusal tokens still frequently appear among the candidate tokens. To adaptively adjust the selection probabilities of these tokens, AdaCD extracts refusal token distribution via an extreme system prompt and designs an adaptive decoding mode switch mechanism to adjust it. Experiments confirm that AdaCD effectively mitigates over-refusal while maintaining safety.

\section{Limitations}
This study has the following limitations:
(1) Fixed hyperparameters $\lambda$ and $\alpha$ settings. Although we analyzed that AdaCD achieves optimal performance when $\lambda=0.9$ and $\alpha=4.5$, allowing these hyperparameters to adaptively vary based on user queries could further improve AdaCD's performance.
(2) Due to our limited computational resources, it remains unclear how AdaCD would perform on models with 70B parameters or larger. However, according to \cite{shi2024navigating}, AdaCD is expected to maintain superior performance when applied to larger-scale models, because the size of the model does not have a direct correlation with the refusal ratio (see Section 5.3 in \cite{shi2024navigating}). (3) We did not conduct human evaluations or keyword-based evaluations because human evaluation is highly subjective and its results are difficult to reproduce, while the significant differences in model vocabularies make it impossible to use a unified keyword-based evaluation standard. Therefore, we employed the state-of-the-art evaluation tool WildGuard to assess the responses from a semantic perspective. As an alternative, we provide examples from our experiments in Appendix \ref{case-study} for intuitive comparison.

\section{Ethical Considerations}
All models and datasets used are open-source. However, the datasets employed for evaluating malicious queries may contain certain unethical or illegal data. We ensure that these datasets are used solely for academic research purposes. Moreover, our goal is to enhance the safety of large language models, and we strongly advocate for the responsible use of our approach in research and other applications.

Figure \ref{alpha} illustrates the influence of $\alpha$ on the refusal ratio under over-refusal and malicious scenarios. \section{Sensitivity Analysis}
\label{sensitivity-analysis}
\subsection{Adaptive Plausibility Constraint Parameter $\beta$}
\label{apc-beta}
Following \cite{li2023contrastive}, AdaCD also adopts the Adaptive Plausibility Constraint to prevent the model from generating grammatically incorrect outputs. Specifically, AdaCD selects the next token only from a confidence-filtered candidate token set $\mathcal{W}$, which is defined as:
\[
\mathcal{W} = \left\{ y_n \in \mathcal{V} \;\middle|\; P_{\pi}(y_n \mid x, y_{<n}) \ge \beta \cdot \max_{y \in \mathcal{V}} P_{\pi}(y \mid x, y_{<n}) \right\}.
\]
where $\mathcal{V}$ denotes the vocabulary of the model. As described in Section \ref{key-ob}, when facing over-refusal scenarios, our objective is to filter out low-probability non-refusal tokens from the candidate list. Therefore, we set $\beta$ to 0.01 instead of the default value of 0.1 used in \cite{li2023contrastive}. To investigate the impact of the parameter $\beta$ on AdaCD, we vary $\beta$ among 0, 0.01, 0.05, and the default value of 0.1. Table \ref{ab-beta} reports the refusal ratios of AdaCD under different $\beta$ settings. When $\beta$ is set to 0, the Adaptive Plausibility Constraint is removed. As shown in Table \ref{ab-beta}, except for the case of $\beta = 0$, increasing $\beta$ consistently leads to higher refusal ratios in both over-refusal and malicious scenarios. For $\beta = 0$, we observe that the model frequently generates grammatically incorrect outputs, making the evaluation results unreliable. To provide a more intuitive analysis, we present three representative examples in Table \ref{ab-beta-case} to illustrate this issue. Notably, this phenomenon also exposes a drawback in the recent work \cite{shi2024navigating}.

\subsection{Applying AdaCD To Top $k$ Tokens }
\label{step-k}
Recent studies have shown that the first $k$ tokens play a critical role in decoding behavior and can significantly influence the generation of subsequent tokens~\cite{xu2024safedecoding,li2025gumiho}. Therefore, in our experiments, we apply AdaCD only to the first 10 token positions using adaptive contrastive decoding, while greedy decoding is employed for the remaining tokens. To investigate the impact of applying AdaCD to the first $k$ positions, we further vary $k$ among 5, 20, 40, and the maximum number of new tokens $N$. Table \ref{ab-k} reports the refusal ratios under different values of $k$. As shown in Table \ref{ab-k}, AdaCD is relatively insensitive to the parameter $k$, and its effectiveness slightly improves as $k$ increases. However, increasing $k$ also leads to higher inference complexity. Therefore, we recommend using $k = 10$ as a general hyperparameter.

\section{Algorithm Workflow}
\label{algorithm-workflow}
To provide a clearer understanding of AdaCD, we present the pseudo-code in Algorithm \ref{code}. Overall, our AdaCD is training-free, requiring no modification of model parameters, and model-agnostic, directly altering the output distribution without changing the model’s architecture. For a given user query \(x\), we first initialize \(x\) as the initial sequence (Alg.~\ref{code}, line 1). Next, a forward pass is performed on both the model with the extreme system prompt $p^*$ and the model without any system prompt (Alg.~\ref{code}, line 4 \& line 6). Within the specified range of contrastive decoding steps \(k\), the top-1 token \(y_n^*\) is obtained from the model with the extreme system prompt (Alg.~\ref{code}, line 8), and the agreement ratio \(\text{agr}(n)\) is computed (Alg.~\ref{code}, line 10). Based on the difference between token distributions, the refusal token distribution is then extracted (Alg.~\ref{code}, line 12). The maximum probability \(\rho\) in the model's current token distribution is compared with the adaptive confidence threshold \(\lambda \cdot \rho^*\) (Alg.~\ref{code}, line 14 - line 15). 
If \(\text{agr}(n) \ge \lambda\) and \(\rho \ge \lambda \cdot \rho^*\), the refusal token distribution is added to the current token distribution to increase the probability of selecting refusal tokens; otherwise, it is subtracted to suppress the probability of selecting refusal tokens (Alg.~\ref{code}, line 16 - line 20). Finally, for the remaining \(N-k\) tokens, standard decoding is used to generate the rest output (Alg.~\ref{code}, line 24).

\begin{algorithm}[h]
\caption{Adaptive Contrastive Decoding (AdaCD)}
\label{code}

\KwIn{User query $x$, extreme system prompt $p^*$, contrastive steps $k$,\\ \qquad \quad hyperparameters $\alpha$, $\lambda$, max new token number $N$}
\KwOut{Generated sequence $y$}
\BlankLine
$y \leftarrow x$\;
\For{$n \leftarrow 1$ \KwTo $N$}{
        \textcolor{commentgreen}{\tcp{Forward passes}}
        $P_{\pi}(y_n|x, y_{<n}) \leftarrow \sigma( f_{\pi}(y_n \mid x, y_{<n}))$\;
    \eIf{$n \le k$}{
        $P_{\pi}(y_n|p^*, x, y_{<n}) \leftarrow \sigma( f_{\pi}(y_n \mid p^*, x, y_{<n}))$\;
        
        \textcolor{commentgreen}{\tcp{Calculate Agreement Ratio}}

        $y_{n}^\ast \leftarrow \arg\max_{y_n}\big(P_{\pi}(y_n|p^*,x, y_{<n})\big)$\;
        
        $\mathrm{rank} \leftarrow \text{The index of } y^*_n \text{ in sorted } P_{\pi}(y_n|x, y_{<n})$\;
        $\mathrm{agr}(n) \leftarrow 1 / \mathrm{rank}$\;
        \textcolor{commentgreen}{\tcp{Refusal Token Distribution Extracting}}
        $\Delta P_n \leftarrow \sigma( f_{\pi}(y_n \mid p^*, x, y_{<n})-f_{\pi}(y_n \mid x, y_{<n}))$\;
        
        \textcolor{commentgreen}{\tcp{Adaptive Decoding Mode Switch}}
         $\rho \leftarrow \max_{y_n} P_{\pi}(y_n \mid x, y_{<n})$\;
        $\rho^* \leftarrow P_{\pi}(y^*_n \mid p^*, x, y_{<n})$\;  
        \eIf{$\mathrm{agr}(n) \ge \lambda$ \textbf{and} $ \rho \ge \lambda \cdot\rho^*$}{
            $P^*_{\pi}(y_n \mid x, y_{<n}) \leftarrow  P_{\pi}(y_n|p^*, x, y_{<n})) + \alpha \cdot \Delta P_n$\;
        }
        {
            $P^*_{\pi}(y_n \mid x, y_{<n}) \leftarrow  P_{\pi}(y_n|p^*, x, y_{<n}) - \alpha \cdot \Delta P_n$\;
        }
        $y_n \leftarrow \arg\max_{y_n} P^*_{\pi}(y_n \mid x, y_{<n})$\;}
    {
        \textcolor{commentgreen}{\tcp{Fall back to standard decoding}}
        $y_{n} \leftarrow \arg\max_{y_n}\big(P_{\pi}(y_n|x, y_{<n})\big)$\;
    }
    
    Update sequence $y \leftarrow y \cup \{y_n\}$\;
}
\Return $y$\;
\end{algorithm}
